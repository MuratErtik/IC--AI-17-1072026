Document 1: Origins of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

The term "Retrieval-Augmented Generation" was formally introduced in a 2020 
research paper by Patrick Lewis and collaborators at Facebook AI Research 
(FAIR), in collaboration with University College London and New York 
University. The paper, titled "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for 
Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks," proposed combining a pre-trained 
sequence-to-sequence language model with a dense vector retrieval system 
over an external corpus (in their case, a Wikipedia-based index).

Before this formalization, similar ideas existed in earlier information 
retrieval research, including open-domain question answering systems 
from the mid-2010s that combined a search step with a reading 
comprehension model. However, RAG as a unified architecture — where 
retrieval and generation are optimized somewhat jointly and the retrieved 
passages are fed directly into the generation context — is credited to 
the 2020 FAIR paper.

RAG adoption accelerated significantly starting in 2022-2023, driven by 
two factors: the rise of large language models (LLMs) with strong 
in-context reasoning ability, and the commercial need to ground LLM 
outputs in private, up-to-date, or proprietary enterprise data without 
retraining the underlying model.

Source: Lewis et al., "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for 
Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks," NeurIPS 2020.
